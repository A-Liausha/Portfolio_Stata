# Semiconductor Shock: An Event Study of U.S. Export Controls on Chinese Firms
## Executive Summary
On October 7, 2022, the U.S. implemented export controls restricting Chinese access to advanced semiconductors. Using the Market Model as a baseline, with Mean-Adjusted and Market-Adjusted models as robustness checks, we estimated cumulative abnormal returns (CARs) for Chinese semiconductor firms over three event windows: (-1,+1), (-5,+5), and (-10,+10). All three models produced statistically significant negative CARs across all windows. Under the baseline Market Model, the (-1,+1) window yielded a mean CAR of -4.65% (p<0.001), the (-5,+5) window -3.57% (p<0.001), and the (-10,+10) window -2.62% (p<0.05). The strongest and most uniform reaction occurred immediately post-announcement, with partial mean reversion in the longer window. I recommend accelerating domestic R&D for advanced nodes, diversifying supply chains away from U.S. technology, and improving geopolitical risk communication.

## Question
What is the financial risk of U.S. export restrictions to our semiconductor business, and where should we reallocate our product portfolio?
## Methodology 
STATA: Market Model, Mean-Adjusted and Market-Adjusted models to estimate cumulative abnormal returns over (-1,+1), (-5,+5), and (-10,+10) event windows around October 7, 2022, based on a 180-day estimation period, and validated results with t-tests, Wilcoxon, and sign tests.
## Skills 
Stata: xtset, bysort, egen, forvalues, merge, event study models, reg/predict, ttest, signrank, signtest, date functions, and missing value handling

## Results and Recommendations
Using the Market Model as our primary analytical framework, validated by two alternative models, we found that the U.S. export controls erased an average of 4.65% of shareholder value within three days for Chinese semiconductor firms, with nearly eight out of ten companies losing ground. The damage held steady through the first week, but by the second week the market began distinguishing between winners and losers: companies exposed to advanced chips continued to struggle, while others partially recovered. This tells us that investors do not treat all semiconductor firms equally under U.S. restrictions.

The October 2022 export controls represent a structural shift, not a one-time shock. Chinese semiconductor firms must adapt strategically, and investors should price in persistent geopolitical risk. Based on these results, I recommend Chinese semiconductor companies: 1) accelerate domestic R&D for advanced nodes while building parallel mature-node revenue streams to fund long-term innovation, 2) diversify supply chains away from American technology, and 3) enhance investor communication around geopolitical risk management to protect stock price stability during future policy shocks.
